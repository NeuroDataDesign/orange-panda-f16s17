Signal Processing

  • Extracting Spikes from raw signals
  • Extracting Features for classification
  • Applicable with EEG, ECoG, and MEG

Spike Sorting

  • Invasive Approaches
    • Isolate and extract spikes emitted by a single neuron (per recording electrode)​
    • Multiunit (Neural) Hash
      • Spikes from neighboring neurons -->
        • Higher amplitude deflections in recorded signal

Simple Method

  • Peak Amplitude
    • Strong signals from neurons at different distances
      • But can be identical for different neurons
  • Window Discriminator
    • Experimenter visually examines data
    • Places windows on aligned, similar spikes
      • All spikes crossing 1+ windows is same neuron

Trend

  • Cluster spikes into group based on shape
  • Each group = spikes from 1 neuron

Frequency Domain Analysis

  • Non invasive
    • Captures correlated activities of many neurons
      • Repetitive activity
  • Fourier Analysis
    • Convert a signal into a weighted sum of sine and cosine waves
      • Of different frequencies / ampltudes

Example

  • Overt and Imagined Movements
    • Power changes in:
      • Mu (8-12 Hz)
      • Beta (13-30 Hz)
      • Gamma (>30 Hz)

Fourier Expansion

  • Expansion of period of periodic signal s(t)
    • Sum of infinite terms
  • Synthesis of signals
    • Sum up sin/cos functions with different weights (a/b)
  • Analysis of signals
    • Weights (a/b) are calculated from input signals
      • Also referred to as amplitudes

Applications

  • Filtering based on frequency
    • EEG signals are often corrupted by:
      • Line noise
      • 60 Hz in US from
        • 60 Hz AC power supply
      • Filtered using a "notch" filter

Fourier amplitudes

What's happening

  • Correlation between input s(t) and a sin/cos wave
    • Of a given frequency
  • The similarity is captured by a(n) and b(n)

Fourier series

  • Fourier transform of signal s(t)

Discrete Fourier Transform

  • Time series sampled at time points t = 0,..., T-1

Discrete Fourier Transform

  • Amplitude / Phase Spectrum
  • BCI
    • Focus on magnitude of changes in different frequencies (based on task)

Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)

  • Speed Difference
    • DFT = T^2 mathematical operations
    • FFT = T long T mathematical operations
  • Cooley-Tukey Algorithm
    • Repetitively breaks down a DFT into smaller DFTs

Applications

  • Extract power spectrum over a time interval (FFT)
    • Power in given frequency is analyzed
  • Ex. Motor movement or imagery
    • Reduces power in Mu frequency band
      • (8-12 Hz)
  • Ex. Subject-specific frequencies
    • Subject performs movements
    • Frequency bands with robust changes in power
    • BCI through movement imagery

Applications

  • Use features bank
    • Machine Learning Algorithm selects features
      • Enhance classification accuracy on test data

Fourier Analysis Downsides

  • Representing finite, non-repetitive signals
  • Showing sharp peaks and discontinuities
  • Assumes stationary signal
    • Brain's statistical properties change

Short Term Fourier

  • STFT
  • Fourier analysis over short time windows
    • Small window = good temporal, poor frequency
    • Large window = poor temporal, good freq.

Solution: Wavelet Transform

  • Wavelet = finite bases function
    • Scaled + translated copy of mother wavelet
      • Mother wavelet = single finite length waveform
  • Represents original signal as linear combo of wavelets
    • Signal is analysed using appropriate wavelet coeff.

Normalized Slope Descriptors

For Time Varying Signals

  • A.k.a. Hjorth parameters
    • Mean Power (activity)
      • a(0) = Signal variance in epoch
    • Root-Mean-Square Frequency (mobility)
      • a(2) = Signal's 1st derivative variance
    • Root-Mean-Square Frequency Spread (Complexity)
      • a(4) = Signal's 1st derivative variance

Benefits of Hjorth Parameters

For Time Varying Signals

  • Based on variance
    • Much faster to compute
  • Used for:
    • Ongoing characterization of time varying signals

Fractal Dimension

  • Fractal = property of self-similarity
    • Repetition of parts of a signal throughout
    • Quantitative measure of self-similarity

Autoregressive Model

  • Natural signals usually correlated (over time or space)
    • Allows to predict based on past measurements
  • Assumes signal properties are stationary
    • E = zero mean white noise
      • accounts for diff. btwn. signal and linear weighed sum approximation
    • p = order of AR model
      • window of past inputs used to predict

Adaptive Autoregressive Model

  • Includes a time-varying set of coefficients a (i,t)

  • a (i,t) updated using recursive least-squares optimization
    • Kalman filtering
    • Captures local statistical structure of signal over time

Bayesian Filtering

  • Estimates signal properties and their uncertainty
  • Bayes' Rule
    • P(x|y) = posterior probability of x given y
    • P(y|x) = likelihood
    • P(x) = prior probability of x

Bayesian Filtering Example

  • BCI = find cause of measured EEG signal
    • Estimate posterior probability P(stimulus|EEG)
      • Hard to do directly
    • Estimate probability P(EEG|Stimulus)
      • Expose subject to stimuli and collect stimulus-response data
    • Prior Probability
      • Fixed or estimated from data

Bayesian Filtering Equation

  • Info from new measurement y(t)
    • Combined with previous posterior
      • P( x (t-1)| y(1) ,..., y(t-1))
  • Obtain new posterior distribution at time (t)
  • Probabilistic inferences in a:
    • Dynamic Bayesian Network

Kalman Filter

  • Recursively updates:
    • mean x(t) | covariance s(t)
    • prediction of: mean x-bar(t) | covariance M(t)
    • prediction error y(t) - B(x-bar(t))
    • correction term K(t) [ y(t) - B(x-bar(t)) ]
    • kalman gain K(t)
      • ​weight given to new evidence y(t)
        • ​function of noise covariances Q / R

Kalman Filter

  • Assumes dyamics and measurement processes are:
    • Linear
    • Gaussian
    • Not true in all real world examples

Particle Filtering

  • Allows for estimation of posterior distribution over hidden states
    • Non-linear
    • Non-Gaussian
  • Same equation as for Kalman Filter
    • Except:
      • Posterior P(x(t) | y(1), ..., y(t)
      • Estimated using a population of samples (particles)

Prediction Re-sampling Steps

  • Completed at each time step
  • Starting population (N)
    • Usually 1,000-5,000
  • From prior distribution p(x(0))

Spatial Filtering

  • Enhancing local activity
  • Reducing noise common to all channels
  • Decreasing data dimensionality
  • Identifying hidden cources
  • Finding projections to maximize discrimination
    • Between different classes

BiPolar

  • Continuous electrical brain signals
  • Highlight electrical potentials differences between two electrodes (i and j)
    • S = s(i) - s(j)

Laplacian

  • Local activity at electrode i
    • Subtracts average activity in 4 orthogonal nearest neighbor electrodes

Common Average Referencing

  • Local activity at electrode i
    • Subtracts average over all electrodes

Principal Component Analysis

  • Finds dominant directions of variability in data
  • Reduce dimensionality <<L
  • Decorrelates input
    • Used for classification and other BCI analyses
  • ex. of (N) data points
    • (N) Vectors of signals from L electrodes at time t

Principal Component Analysis

  • Mathematically
  • Matrix A = L x L sample covariance matrix of input
  • Directions of maximal variance
    • Compute eigenvectors of matrix A
    • A*v = (lamda)*v
      • ​Large evalue = most variation
